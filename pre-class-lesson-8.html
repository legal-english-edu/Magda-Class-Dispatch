Topic one

The price of prestige

Somewhere on the outskirts of Milan, on a street called Via del Lavoro – Labour Street – workers were making Dior handbags. They lived where they worked. They had no contracts. Some had no legal right to be in the country at all. The bags they produced sold for €2,500. The workshop charged Dior €53 to make each one.

This is not a story about counterfeit goods. It is a story about real ones.

In June 2024, a Milan court placed Manufactures Dior SRL – an Italian subsidiary of LVMH, the world's largest luxury group – under judicial administration. Investigators had found that Dior's subcontractors were using undocumented migrant workers in conditions the court described as below the minimum required by any ethical standard. The court was clear that this was not an isolated failure: the violations were systematic across the industry, driven by the structural pressure to cut costs while maintaining the mythology of craftsmanship.

The legal instrument used against Dior was striking in its own right. Judicial administration – commissioning an external authority to run a company – was introduced into Italian law in 1992, in the aftermath of the murders of judges Giovanni Falcone and Paolo Borsellino. It was an anti-Mafia tool. Its application here, against one of the most recognisable luxury brands in the world, was not accidental.

Dior's defence was straightforward: it conducted regular audits; its suppliers had concealed what was happening; the brand itself was, in a sense, a victim of deception. Prosecutors saw it differently. The question they were effectively asking is one you may recognise: at what point does a failure to know become a choice not to know?

By May 2025, the matter was resolved. Italy's competition authority closed its investigation after Dior agreed to pay €2 million over five years to combat worker exploitation and tighten supply chain oversight. The judicial administration order was lifted. LVMH's annual revenue exceeds €80 billion. The €2 million commitment – spread across five years – is, in financial terms, a rounding error.

No one was convicted. The bags are still in the windows.

Key facts
  • Dior handbags sold for €2,500; subcontractors charged €53 per bag to produce them
  • Workers in Milan-area workshops were undocumented, living on-site, without contracts
  • The Milan court used judicial administration – originally an anti-Mafia measure – against Dior
  • Armani, Valentino, Loro Piana, and around a dozen other luxury brands faced similar probes in the same period
  • Dior paid €2 million to close the investigation; LVMH's annual revenue exceeds €80 billion
  • Italy's competition authority found no wrongdoing – after accepting Dior's voluntary commitments

For Thursday

Discussion questions

01
Dior argued its suppliers had hidden the exploitation despite regular audits. As a prosecutor, would that defence hold? At what point does a failure to investigate become a choice not to know?
02
The fine was €2 million against a company with €80 billion in annual revenue. If the penalty is smaller than the saving, is it a punishment – or a business cost?
Vocabulary

Core vocabulary for Thursday

Before class
These are the key terms for Thursday's discussion. Read through them before class.
due diligence /djuː ˈdɪlɪdʒəns/
należyta staranność
The reasonable steps a company is legally and ethically expected to take to identify and prevent harm within its operations and supply chain.
The court found that the company had failed to exercise due diligence in monitoring its suppliers. [legal/business]
Sąd stwierdził, że spółka nie dochowała należytej staranności w nadzorowaniu swoich dostawców.
judicial administration
zarząd komisaryczny / zarząd sądowy
A court order placing a company under external supervision, typically where internal governance has failed. In Italy, drawn from anti-Mafia law.
The Milan court ordered the company into judicial administration after investigators uncovered systematic labour violations. [legal]
Sąd w Mediolanie ustanowił zarząd komisaryczny po tym, jak śledczy ujawnili systemowe naruszenia prawa pracy.
to commission
powierzać / zlecać
To formally appoint or authorise someone to carry out a task or role, especially one of oversight or control.
The court commissioned an independent authority to oversee the company's operations until compliance was restored. [legal/formal]
Sąd powierzył niezależnemu organowi nadzór nad działalnością spółki do czasu przywrócenia zgodności z przepisami.
oversight /ˈəʊvəsaɪt/
nadzór
The act of supervising or monitoring an activity, organisation, or process to ensure it is carried out correctly.
The ruling found a systemic failure of oversight at every level of the supply chain. [legal/formal]
Orzeczenie stwierdziło systemowe zaniedbania w zakresie nadzoru na każdym szczeblu łańcucha dostaw.
subcontractor
podwykonawca
A company hired by a main contractor to carry out part of a larger job; in law, the question is how far the main contractor's liability extends.
Dior argued it had no direct knowledge of the conditions at its subcontractors' workshops. [legal/commercial]
Dior twierdził, że nie miał bezpośredniej wiedzy o warunkach panujących w warsztatach swoich podwykonawców.
to subcontract
zlecać podwykonawstwo
To hire another company to carry out work that you have been contracted to do yourself.
The production had been subcontracted several layers deep, making it difficult to establish where responsibility lay. [legal/commercial]
Produkcja została zlecona podwykonawcom na kilku poziomach, co utrudniało ustalenie, gdzie leżała odpowiedzialność.
wilful blindness
umyślna nieświadomość / świadome ignorowanie faktów
A legal doctrine holding that a person or organisation cannot claim ignorance of facts they deliberately avoided knowing.
The prosecution argued that the company's failure to investigate amounted to wilful blindness. [legal]
Oskarżenie argumentowało, że zaniechanie przez spółkę prowadzenia dochodzeń stanowiło umyślną nieświadomość.
to turn a blind eye
przymykać oko
To deliberately ignore something you know is wrong.
Investigators alleged that brand executives had effectively turned a blind eye to conditions their own audit system should have flagged. [informal/journalistic]
Śledczy twierdzili, że kierownictwo marki przymykało oko na warunki, które powinny zostać wykryte przez własny system audytów.
voluntary commitment
dobrowolne zobowiązanie
A formal promise made to a regulator to avoid prosecution or further legal action; not a criminal admission, but not exoneration either.
The regulator closed its investigation after accepting a voluntary commitment from the brand to improve supply chain oversight. [legal/regulatory]
Organ regulacyjny zamknął postępowanie po przyjęciu od marki dobrowolnego zobowiązania do poprawy nadzoru nad łańcuchem dostaw.
to settle
zawrzeć ugodę / uregulować sprawę
To resolve a legal dispute by agreement, typically involving payment, without proceeding to trial or conviction.
The company chose to settle with the regulator rather than face the reputational damage of a prolonged investigation. [legal]
Spółka zdecydowała się zawrzeć ugodę z organem regulacyjnym, zamiast narażać się na szkody wizerunkowe związane z długotrwałym dochodzeniem.
supply chain
łańcuch dostaw
The sequence of companies and processes involved in producing and delivering a product, from raw materials to the final consumer.
The scandal exposed how little visibility luxury brands maintain over their supply chains. [business]
Skandal ujawnił, jak niewielską kontrolę marki luksusowe sprawują nad swoimi łańcuchami dostaw.
liability /ˌlaɪəˈbɪlɪti/
odpowiedzialność prawna
Legal responsibility for something, especially for paying compensation or facing punishment.
The central legal question was how far the brand's liability extended beyond its directly employed workforce. [legal]
Kluczowe pytanie prawne dotyczyło tego, jak daleko odpowiedzialność prawna marki sięgała poza jej bezpośrednio zatrudnionych pracowników.
to be liable /ˈlaɪəbl/
ponosić odpowiedzialność prawną
To be legally responsible for something.
The question was whether the parent company could be held liable for violations committed by its subsidiaries' suppliers. [legal]
Pytanie brzmiało, czy spółka dominująca może ponosić odpowiedzialność prawną za naruszenia popełnione przez dostawców jej spółek zależnych.
exploitation /ˌeksplɔɪˈteɪʃən/
wyzysk
The act of treating people unfairly in order to benefit from their work, typically by paying them little or denying them basic rights.
The court described the working conditions as exploitation of undocumented migrants. [legal/journalistic]
Sąd opisał warunki pracy jako wyzysk nielegalnych imigrantów.
to exploit
wyzyskiwać
To treat someone unfairly in order to benefit from their work or situation.
The subcontractors had systematically exploited workers who had no legal status and no recourse. [legal/journalistic]
Podwykonawcy systematycznie wyzyskiwali pracowników, którzy nie mieli statusu prawnego ani możliwości dochodzenia swoich praw.
provenance /ˈprɒvənəns/
pochodzenie / proweniencja
The place of origin or source of something; in luxury goods, a term used to assert authenticity and quality of manufacture.
The brand's marketing emphasised the provenance of its materials, while the reality of its supply chain told a different story. [formal/commercial]
Marketing marki podkreślał pochodzenie jej materiałów, podczas gdy rzeczywistość łańcucha dostaw mówiła co innego.
craftsmanship /ˈkrɑːftsmənʃɪp/
rzemiosło / kunszt rzemieślniczy
Skill in making things by hand; in luxury marketing, the quality of expert artisanal production – often invoked to justify high prices.
The brand had built its reputation on craftsmanship, a claim that became harder to sustain once the supply chain was examined. [commercial/journalistic]
Marka zbudowała swoją reputację na kunszcie rzemieślniczym – twierdzeniu, które stało się trudniejsze do utrzymania po zbadaniu łańcucha dostaw.
undocumented
nieudokumentowany / bez dokumentów
Lacking the official documents required for legal residence or employment in a country.
Several of the workers found in the workshop were undocumented, with no contracts and no legal right to remain in the country. [legal/journalistic]
Niektórzy pracownicy znalezieni w warsztacie byli nieudokumentowani – bez umów i bez prawa do pobytu w kraju.
to audit /ˈɔːdɪt/
przeprowadzać audyt / kontrolować
To carry out a formal inspection of an organisation's accounts, processes, or compliance with rules and standards.
The company claimed it had regularly audited its suppliers, but the inspections had failed to detect any violations. [business/legal]
Spółka twierdziła, że regularnie przeprowadzała audyty swoich dostawców, jednak kontrole nie wykryły żadnych naruszeń.
deterrent /dɪˈterənt/
środek odstraszający
Something that discourages a particular action or behaviour by making the consequences unpleasant enough to avoid.
A fine of €2 million against a company with €80 billion in revenue functions as no deterrent at all. [legal/journalistic]
Grzywna w wysokości 2 milionów euro wobec spółki z przychodami rzędu 80 miliardów euro nie stanowi żadnego środka odstraszającego.
Article vocabulary

Words and phrases from the article

While reading
These terms appear in the Law Society Journal article. Read through them before or alongside the article.
sham
pozorowany / fikcyjny
Something that appears real or legitimate but is not; a deliberate pretence. In this context, a company that exists on paper to create legal distance from the actual wrongdoing.
The investigation revealed a network of sham subcontractors – companies with no real operations, created solely to obscure who was employing the workers. [journalistic/legal]
Śledztwo ujawniło sieć fikcyjnych podwykonawców – spółek bez rzeczywistej działalności, powołanych wyłącznie po to, by ukryć, kto zatrudniał pracowników.
off the books
na czarno / bez rejestracji
Employed or paid without official records – no contract, no tax, no social security contributions. Invisible to any audit or inspection.
Ten of the workers found during the inspection were working off the books, with no employment records of any kind. [informal/legal]
Dziesięciu z pracowników wykrytych podczas kontroli pracowało na czarno – bez jakichkolwiek akt zatrudnienia.
to entrust (something) to
powierzać (coś) komuś
To give someone responsibility for something valuable or important. Carries an implication of continued accountability – you cannot fully hand off responsibility by entrusting.
The fashion house had entrusted production to an external company, but that did not absolve it of responsibility for what happened next. [formal]
Dom mody powierzył produkcję zewnętrznej firmie, ale nie zwalniało go to z odpowiedzialności za to, co nastąpiło później.
multi-layered subcontracting
wielopoziomowe podwykonawstwo
A chain in which work is passed down through several companies in sequence – each one subcontracting to the next – creating increasing distance between the brand at the top and the workers at the bottom.
The prosecution argued that multi-layered subcontracting was not an accident of business structure but a deliberate strategy to avoid liability. [legal/commercial]
Oskarżenie argumentowało, że wielopoziomowe podwykonawstwo nie było przypadkiem struktury biznesowej, lecz celowym działaniem mającym na celu uniknięcie odpowiedzialności.
organisational fault (colpa organizzativa)
wina organizacyjna
An Italian legal concept: a company is held responsible for violations within its supply chain if its internal controls were inadequate, even where the direct wrongdoer was a subcontractor several layers removed.
Under the doctrine of organisational fault, the brand could not simply point to its subcontractors and walk away – the failure of its own systems made it responsible. [Italian/EU law]
Zgodnie z koncepcją winy organizacyjnej marka nie mogła po prostu wskazać na swoich podwykonawców i umywać rąk – to zaniedbania jej własnych systemów czyniły ją odpowiedzialną.
memorandum of understanding
protokół ustaleń / list intencyjny
A non-binding agreement between parties setting out intended cooperation or shared goals. It signals intention but carries no legal force if ignored.
The industry signed a memorandum of understanding on supply chain transparency, though critics noted it created no enforceable obligations. [legal/diplomatic]
Branża podpisała protokół ustaleń dotyczący przejrzystości łańcucha dostaw, choć krytycy zauważyli, że nie stwarza on żadnych egzekwowalnych zobowiązań.
non-binding
niewiążący
Having no legal force; parties may agree in principle but face no legal consequences for failing to comply.
The agreement was explicitly non-binding, which is why several observers questioned what it was actually intended to achieve. [legal]
Porozumienie było wyraźnie niewiążące, dlatego kilku obserwatorów zakwestionowało, co właściwie miało osiągnąć.
misleading commercial practice
wprowadzająca w błąd praktyka handlowa
A specific legal category under Italian and EU consumer law: making false or deceptive claims to consumers about a product, its origin, or its production conditions.
The authority found that the brand's sustainability claims constituted a misleading commercial practice under the Italian Consumer Code. [EU/Italian law]
Organ stwierdził, że twierdzenia marki dotyczące zrównoważonego rozwoju stanowiły wprowadzającą w błąd praktykę handlową w rozumieniu włoskiego kodeksu konsumenckiego.
to retail (at / for)
sprzedawać się detalicznie (za)
Used without an object to describe the price at which something is sold to the end consumer in shops.
The jacket retails for $4,000 in stores; it cost $149 to produce. [commercial]
Kurtka sprzedaje się detalicznie za 4 000 dolarów; jej wyprodukowanie kosztowało 149 dolarów.
to dampen
osłabiać / studzić
To reduce the strength, enthusiasm, or intensity of something.
US tariffs on European goods have dampened American consumers' appetite for luxury spending. [journalistic]
Amerykańskie cła na towary europejskie osłabiły apetyt amerykańskich konsumentów na wydatki na dobra luksusowe.
woes
kłopoty / troski / niedole
Troubles or difficulties, typically multiple and ongoing. Slightly literary; common in journalism.
The company's woes extended beyond Italy – a Bloomberg investigation had also raised questions about its supply chain in Peru. [journalistic/literary]
Kłopoty spółki sięgały dalej niż Włochy – dochodzenie Bloomberga rodziło również pytania dotyczące jej łańcucha dostaw w Peru.
lustre /ˈlʌstə/
blask / połysk
A gentle sheen or glow; used figuratively to mean appeal, prestige, or reputation. "Losing its lustre" means a brand or industry is losing the aura that made it desirable.
After years of scandal, the luxury sector's lustre has faded considerably in the eyes of younger consumers. [journalistic/figurative]
Po latach skandali blask sektora luksusowego znacznie przygasl w oczach młodszych konsumentów.
to persevere
wytrwać / kontynuować mimo trudności
To continue steadfastly in a course of action despite obstacles or discouragement.
The Milan prosecutor's office has shown a willingness to persevere with these investigations despite considerable industry resistance. [formal]
Prokuratura w Mediolanie wykazała gotowosc do kontynuowania tych dochodzeń pomimo znacznego oporu ze strony branży.
