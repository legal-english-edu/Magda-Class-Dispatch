dispatch/dɪˈspætʃ/
noun
1. A message or report sent quickly to its destination – especially one sent by a journalist from abroad, or between military officers.
2. The act of sending someone or something promptly to a particular place.

One Thing From Today
The distinction between tax and duty is one that Polish speakers frequently blur in English. Tax is levied on income, profit, or value; duty is levied on goods – at the border (customs duty, import duty) or at the point of sale (excise duty).

The Warrant

The investigation had taken twice as long as anyone had expected. What began as a straightforward tax case had expanded steadily: first into allegations of customs duty fraud, then into a scheme apparently designed to circumvent income tax on a significant scale. The documents were voluminous, and preparing even a summary required double the time the team had budgeted. The whole process was arduous, and more than once Marta had found the shifting jurisdictional boundaries disconcerting.

The man at the centre of it had a constituency office two streets from the courthouse. He had served in parliament for eleven years. Under First Past the Post, his seat was safe. He was also, it turned out, a director of the shell company through which the excise duty had gone unpaid.

Marta called the team together. Citizens needed to trust that the prosecutor's office was professional enough to pursue serious offences regardless of who stood behind them. She had the authority to press charges. She signed the application.

The officers obtained the warrant the following morning and carried out a search of the parliamentary office before the building opened. They concluded the search by noon. The building was quiet. Outside, nobody noticed.

The Catering Question

Paweł had spent the early days of cryptocurrency advising small investors from a rented desk near the Palace of Culture and Science. That was 2014. He had always run things with economy, keeping the overheads low and the decisions quick. When he moved into event consulting, he took the same approach: a lean operation, two part-time assistants, no excess.

The first client he pitched in the new venture was a law firm holding a partners' retreat. Do you offer full catering? they asked. He had not expected the question. He said he would find out. Back at his desk he spent an afternoon trying to make summaries of long documents on food hygiene licensing, venue regulations, and import duty on specialty goods. It was more strenuous than anything he had done in finance.

He called the law firm back and told them honestly: for this event, he could offer just sweets and coffee. A full spread was beyond his current scope. He expected them to look elsewhere.

Instead, they booked him. The last caterer, the managing partner said, promised everything and delivered nothing. Your economical movement through the problem impressed us. He paused. People who think before they speak usually get it right. In the background, songs by Maanam drifted from another room.
