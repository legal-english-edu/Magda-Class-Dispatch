Poland's crypto crisis

A liquidity scandal, a political veto, and a million customers

Zondacrypto – formerly known as BitBay – is Poland's largest cryptocurrency exchange, claiming over a million customers, around 75% of them Polish. This week it became the subject of a major investigative story.

Since December 2025, users have been reporting problems withdrawing their money: requests left pending for hours or days. An independent analysis found that the exchange's Bitcoin reserves – held in wallets used for day-to-day withdrawals – fell by 99.7% between August 2024 and March 2026. On 1 April 2026, those reserves stood at the equivalent of around 21,000 PLN. The exchange claims to serve half a million active users.

The CEO disputes the figure, saying the company holds significant reserves in offline storage not visible to external analysts – but has provided no evidence to support this, and has refused to publish any proof of reserves, saying the company will not submit to media pressure over sensitive operational data. Meanwhile, sponsored sports clubs have gone unpaid for months, the company has laid off 35% of staff in two departments, and tax arrears have been reported in Estonia, where the company is registered.

The political dimension is significant. Poland's governing coalition has pointed to President Nawrocki's two vetoes of the crypto assets bill – in December 2025 and February 2026 – as having blocked the legal framework that would have brought the market under proper supervision. Without that law, Poland's financial regulator has no jurisdiction over Zondacrypto, which operates under an Estonian licence controlled by a Swiss holding company. The EU's MiCA regulation requires domestic implementation; the deadline is 1 July 2026. Poland ranks 9th among 34 European countries for money laundering and terrorism financing risk.

Key points
  • Poland's largest crypto exchange may have a serious liquidity problem – its accessible Bitcoin reserves have fallen by 99.7% in under two years.
  • The CEO disputes this but has provided no evidence and refuses to publish proof of reserves.
  • The exchange is registered in Estonia and controlled from Switzerland – Poland's regulator has no jurisdiction.
  • Two presidential vetoes have blocked the law that would have created a Polish licensing framework.
  • The exchange has political connections to the right-wing opposition.
  • The EU deadline for implementing crypto regulation is 1 July 2026.
For discussion
  1. The CEO refuses to publish proof of reserves, calling it sensitive operational data. Is that a legitimate business position – or does it tell you everything you need to know?
  2. Zondacrypto is legally registered in Estonia and supervised there. If Polish customers lose money, who is responsible – the company, the Estonian regulator, or the Polish state that failed to pass its own law?
  3. The exchange has clear political connections to one side of Polish politics. Does that change how you read the story – or is it irrelevant to the financial question?

Topic 2 of 2

What's in your food – and who decides

Regulation, trade secrets, and the price of cheaper ingredients

The food on supermarket shelves in Poland and the food sold under the same brand name in the United States are often not the same product. The multinational companies that make them have reformulated their recipes for different markets – not because local tastes differ, but because local rules do. This is a rational corporate response to regulatory asymmetry: use cheaper inputs where the system does not penalise you for doing so.

The gap exists because the US and the EU operate on fundamentally different principles. The EU applies the precautionary principle: a new food additive must be proven safe before it enters the food supply. The burden of proof lies with the manufacturer. The US reverses this logic entirely – an ingredient is assumed safe until enough evidence of harm accumulates to justify action, a process that can take decades. The result is that over 3,000 additives currently approved in the US are banned or restricted in Europe.

In the US, companies can assess the safety of their own new ingredients internally, declare them safe, and put them in food without notifying the regulator at all. Around 99% of new food chemicals introduced since 2000 entered the market this way. Much of what packaged food tastes like is engineered by a small number of specialised flavour firms – three companies control over 55% of a global market worth $40 billion a year. Their formulations are classified as trade secrets, which means that when a label reads "natural flavours," it may represent dozens of individual chemical compounds, none of which require disclosure.

These formulations are not simply designed to make food taste good. They are engineered to target what food scientists call the bliss point – the precise ratio of sugar, salt, and fat calibrated to maximise craving and bypass the body's satiety signals. This is, in a formal sense, legal. It is also a textbook example of regulatory capture: an industry that has shaped the rules governing it so thoroughly that the regulator lacks both the legal authority and the budget to act. Poland is protected by EU standards – but the same global brands operate here, and the same questions about transparency and corporate ethics apply.

Key points
  • The same multinational companies sell different versions of the same product in different countries – not because of local taste, but because different markets have different rules.
  • The EU requires proof of safety before a new food additive is approved. The US assumes it is safe until harm is proven – a process that can take decades.
  • In the US, companies can declare their own ingredients safe without telling the regulator. Around 99% of new food chemicals since 2000 entered the market this way.
  • Much of what packaged food tastes like is engineered by three companies most people have never heard of. Their formulations are trade secrets – legally protected from disclosure.
  • These formulations are not just designed to taste good. They are calibrated to override the body's signal that it has eaten enough.
  • Poland is inside the EU regulatory framework – but the same global brands operate here, and the same questions about transparency and corporate ethics apply.
For discussion
  1. If a company sells a cleaner product in Warsaw than in Chicago, has it done anything wrong – legally or morally?
  2. Who should bear the burden of proof: the company that wants to add an ingredient, or the regulator that wants to ban it?
  3. Do you read ingredient labels? Has that ever changed what you bought?
  4. At what point does a legal business practice become a moral failure?
  5. Poland is inside the EU regulatory framework. Does that make you feel protected – or do you think the questions about transparency and corporate ethics still apply here?
Vocabulary
food additive
dodatek do żywności
A substance added to food during production, typically to improve taste, appearance, or shelf life.
Over 3,000 food additives approved in the US are banned or restricted across Europe.
regulatory loophole
luka prawna / regulacyjna
A gap in the law that allows something to happen without proper oversight.
The company exploited a regulatory loophole to introduce the new chemical without any independent safety review.
self-certification
samocertyfikacja
Declaring your own product or process compliant without independent verification.
Self-certification means the company itself decides whether its new ingredient is safe – without ever telling the regulator.
precautionary principle
zasada ostrożności
The rule that a new substance must be proven safe before use, not after harm emerges.
Under the precautionary principle, a manufacturer must demonstrate safety before the ingredient reaches the market.
burden of proof
ciężar dowodu
The obligation to demonstrate that a claim is true.
The central question is who carries the burden of proof – the company introducing an ingredient, or the state trying to ban it.
regulatory capture
przechwycenie regulatora
When the body supposed to regulate an industry ends up serving that industry's interests instead.
Regulatory capture occurs when the regulator is so dependent on the industry it oversees that it can no longer act against it.
trade secret
tajemnica handlowa
Commercially sensitive information legally protected from disclosure.
The flavour formula is classified as a trade secret, which means its exact composition never appears on the label.
bliss point
punkt błogości
The precise combination of sugar, salt, and fat engineered to maximise consumption without triggering satiety.
Food scientists calibrate the bliss point to keep you eating past the moment your body would normally stop.
satiety
sytość
The feeling of fullness; the body's signal to stop eating.
Ultraprocessed foods are specifically engineered to delay or override satiety.
ultraprocessed food
żywność wysokoprzetworzona
Industrially manufactured food containing substances not found in a home kitchen.
Ultraprocessed food now makes up nearly 60% of the average American adult's diet.
to reformulate
zmienić skład / recepturę
To change the composition of a product, typically for a different market.
Kraft reformulated its Mac and Cheese for the European market to avoid a mandatory warning label.
disclosure
ujawnienie, ujawnianie informacji
The act of making previously hidden information available to others.
Trade secret law in the US overrides ingredient transparency laws, meaning full disclosure is not required.
to lobby
lobować
To attempt to influence legislators or regulators on behalf of a commercial interest.
The food industry spends hundreds of millions of dollars lobbying against regulatory changes that would increase production costs.
