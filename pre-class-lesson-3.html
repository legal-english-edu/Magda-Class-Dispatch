Topic one

Europe's move to ban Chinese equipment from critical infrastructure

In January 2026, the European Commission proposed a revised Cybersecurity Act that would force all EU member states to remove equipment from "high-risk" technology suppliers – primarily China's Huawei and ZTE – from critical infrastructure within three years. The proposal covers not just mobile networks but 18 sectors, including energy, water, cloud services, medical devices, and connected vehicles.

Until now, the EU's approach has been voluntary. Since 2020, the Commission has recommended that member states limit their reliance on Chinese telecoms equipment – but only 13 of 27 have acted. Germany still had Huawei providing around 60% of its 5G infrastructure as recently as 2024. The new legislation would make compliance mandatory and give Brussels, rather than national capitals, the power to designate high-risk suppliers and enforce phase-out timetables.

The UK has been ahead of the EU on this. It mandated the removal of Huawei from its 5G networks by the end of 2027 – but the process has been slow and expensive, with BT missing earlier deadlines. The rip-and-replace programme has been blamed for delays to Britain's 5G rollout and cost billions.

The concerns extend beyond telecoms. Modern cars – especially electric vehicles – are equipped with cameras, LiDAR, microphones, and GPS systems that continuously collect data. Several NATO countries have now banned Chinese-made vehicles from military sites, on the grounds that Chinese law requires manufacturers to share data with the state if asked. The EU's proposed legislation would eventually cover connected vehicles too.

China has rejected the proposals, with Huawei calling them a violation of EU principles of non-discrimination and proportionality, and Beijing accusing Europe of "abusing the concept of national security."

Topic two

When American corporations did business with Hitler

The story of how some of America's biggest companies – Ford, General Motors, IBM, Standard Oil, Coca-Cola, ITT – helped finance, equip, and enable the Nazi regime is well-documented but rarely discussed in polite corporate history. Henry Ford was an open antisemite whose writings directly influenced Hitler; his portrait hung in Hitler's office. In 1938, a grateful Hitler awarded Ford the Order of the German Eagle, the most prestigious decoration that could be granted to a non-German.

IBM's punch-card technology helped the Nazis identify and track Jews across occupied Europe. Each concentration camp operated a Hollerith department using IBM machines to catalogue the movement, sorting, and mass execution of inmates. GM and Ford between them controlled 70% of the German car market by 1939 and built the trucks and engines that made the Blitzkrieg possible. After D-Day, American soldiers were bemused to find Ford and GM engines in the German vehicles they captured in Normandy.

The US Ambassador to Nazi Germany, William Dodd, wrote to President Roosevelt in 1936 that "more than a hundred American corporations have subsidiaries here." Standard Oil was supplying synthetic fuel technology. DuPont had intimate links with IG Farben, the chemical corporation centrally involved in Nazi rearmament and later implicated in the Holocaust. ITT owned a quarter of Focke-Wulf, the Luftwaffe aircraft manufacturer – and after the war, successfully claimed $27 million in compensation for Allied bombing damage to the plant.

These companies chose profit over principle – but they also operated in a legal grey zone where none of this was technically illegal. When the full horror of the Holocaust was revealed, they concocted myths of innocence. The country was only too happy to believe them.

Discussion questions

01
The EU spent six years asking member states to remove Chinese equipment voluntarily – and most of them didn't. What does it tell you about European security that it takes mandatory legislation to get countries to act on a known risk?
02
Huawei says the proposed ban violates EU principles of non-discrimination and proportionality. If you were representing them, would you have a case?
03
These companies chose profit over principle – but they operated in a legal grey zone where none of this was technically illegal. Is that an excuse, or is it the whole point?
Vocabulary – Topic one

The technology you can't trust

to phase out
wycofywać stopniowo
To gradually stop using or providing something over a fixed period.
The EU wants member states to phase out all Chinese telecoms equipment within three years.
high-risk vendor
dostawca wysokiego ryzyka
A supplier whose products or services are judged to pose a potential threat to national security.
Huawei has been classified as a high-risk vendor by several European governments.
backdoor
tylne drzwi / furtka
A hidden method of bypassing security controls in a system, often built in deliberately to allow covert access.
Critics say the real danger is not the equipment itself, but the possibility of a backdoor that could be activated remotely.
[computing / intelligence]
to rip out
wyrwać, usunąć całkowicie
To remove something completely and forcibly, especially equipment that is already installed and in use.
BT has spent billions ripping out Huawei components from its 5G network.
[informal]
procurement
zamówienia (publiczne)
The process of obtaining goods or services, especially for a government or large organisation.
The new rules would affect procurement decisions across 18 critical sectors.
[formal]
sovereignty
suwerenność
Supreme authority and independence; here, a state's right to control its own infrastructure and data without foreign interference.
The debate is really about digital sovereignty – who controls the infrastructure that a country depends on.
proportionality
proporcjonalność
The principle that a measure taken by an authority must be appropriate and no more restrictive than necessary to achieve its aim.
Huawei argues that a blanket ban fails the test of proportionality under EU law.
[legal]
to comply (with) / compliance
stosować się do / zgodność z przepisami
To act in accordance with rules, regulations, or demands; the state of doing so.
Only 13 of 27 member states have complied with the Commission's recommendations so far.
[formal]
to embed
osadzić, wbudować
To fix something deeply within a surrounding structure; used literally (technology embedded in a car) and figuratively (risks embedded in a supply chain).
Chinese sensors are embedded in vehicles sold by European manufacturers, not just Chinese ones.
to exfiltrate
wyprowadzić (dane)
To extract data or information covertly from a system or location without authorisation.
The concern is that vehicle data could be exfiltrated to servers controlled by a foreign state.
[intelligence / technical]
leverage
wpływ, przewaga; wykorzystywać
Power or influence used to achieve a desired result; to use something to maximum advantage.
Beijing could use its control of supply chains as leverage in any future geopolitical conflict.
to designate
wyznaczyć, zakwalifikować
To officially assign a status or category to something; here, to formally label a supplier as high-risk.
Under the new Act, the Commission would have the power to designate suppliers as high-risk at EU level.
[formal]
safeguard
zabezpieczenie; chronić
A measure taken to protect against risk or harm; to protect.
Existing safeguards have been voluntary, and most countries have simply ignored them.
LiDAR
LiDAR (wykrywanie i pomiar odległości za pomocą lasera)
Light Detection and Ranging – a technology that uses rapid laser pulses to build a detailed 3D map of the surrounding environment.
A car equipped with LiDAR parked near a military base is, in effect, a mobile three-dimensional scanner.
Vocabulary – Topic two

Doing business with Hitler

complicity
współudział
Involvement in or knowledge of wrongdoing, especially as a partner or accomplice.
The question is not just whether these companies knew, but the depth of their complicity.
to turn a blind eye
przymykać oko
To deliberately ignore something you know is wrong.
Washington turned a blind eye to the dealings because the profits were too large to question.
to divest (from)
wycofać się z inwestycji
To sell off investments or withdraw financial involvement from a company or country.
None of these corporations chose to divest from Germany, even after the persecution of Jews became public.
rearmament
zbrojenia, remilitaryzacja
The process of building up a country's military strength again, especially after a period of disarmament.
German rearmament in the 1930s was funded in part by American capital and technology.
subsidiary
spółka zależna, filia
A company controlled by a larger parent company, especially one operating in a foreign country.
Ford's German subsidiary, Ford-Werke in Cologne, produced military vehicles for the Wehrmacht.
forced labour / slave labour
praca przymusowa / praca niewolnicza
Work extracted from people under threat of punishment, without consent or fair compensation.
Ford's Cologne plant used forced labour between 1941 and 1945, including prisoners of war.
to collude (with)
działać w zmowie
To cooperate secretly with someone for a dishonest or illegal purpose.
Several US firms colluded with the Nazi regime to suppress competition and divide markets.
war profiteering
czerpanie zysków z wojny
Making excessive profits from the sale of goods or services during wartime, especially at the expense of others.
After the war, GM was accused of war profiteering on both sides of the conflict.
to whitewash
wybielac
To deliberately conceal unpleasant or incriminating facts about a person or organisation to protect their reputation.
Corporate histories have systematically whitewashed the extent of American involvement with the Nazis.
carte blanche
wolna ręka, pełna swoboda działania
Complete freedom to act as one wishes, without restriction.
Hitler gave German subsidiaries of US firms virtual carte blanche to operate as they wished, so long as they served the war effort.
[from French, used in English]
moral reckoning
rozliczenie moralne
A moment when a person or institution is forced to confront the ethical consequences of past actions.
For most of these corporations, the moral reckoning never came – they simply moved on.
to bankroll
finansować, sponsorować
To provide the money for a project, enterprise, or activity, especially one that is large or controversial.
American banks effectively bankrolled Hitler's rise to power through loans and investments in German industry.
[informal]
plausible deniability
wiarygodna zaprzeczalność
The ability to deny knowledge of or involvement in something because there is no direct proof linking you to it.
The corporate structure of subsidiaries gave headquarters in the US a degree of plausible deniability.
to fall into line
podporządkować się, dostosować się
To conform to expected behaviour or comply with the demands of those in power.
When the Nazis asked US subsidiaries to dismiss Jewish employees, most companies fell into line without protest.
