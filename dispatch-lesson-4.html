One Thing From Today
When describing the limits of someone's official authority or responsibility, three words come up – and they are not interchangeable. Remit is the natural choice in British professional and institutional English. Competence (uncountable) carries the same meaning in formal legal English: this falls outside the competence of this court. Competences (plural) looks similar but belongs specifically to EU institutional writing, where it refers to the areas of authority assigned to bodies like the Commission or the Council. Outside that context, the plural sounds like a translation.

Marta had spent fifteen years as the sole distributor of a niche German legal software in Poland – prolific in her output, reliable in her contracts, invisible to the press. Then the German parent company dissolved her agreement without notice and released a statement to selected media outlets saying the partnership had ended "by mutual consent."

It had not.

She called her attorney on a Friday afternoon. He listened carefully, then told her that pursuing damages in a German court was, frankly, outside his competence – she would need someone in Munich. He gave her a name.

What followed took two years. The scarcity of lawyers willing to take on a well-resourced German corporation on a contingency basis made the search slow and demoralising. But Marta was patient. She had grown up hearing her grandmother describe the economy of scarcity – queuing for flour, bartering for shoes – and she had learned early that waiting was not the same as losing.

She won. The settlement was never disclosed. The German statement was quietly removed from the internet.

She did not issue one of her own.

The class had been looking at German companies and their relationship with the Nazi state. The list was long and the names were familiar: IG Farben, Krupp, Volkswagen, Deutsche Bank. What was striking was not the exceptions but the pattern – how thoroughly German industry had integrated itself into the regime, and how little of this had interrupted the postwar careers of the companies involved.

Allianz was the afternoon's central example. The company had insured SS facilities from 1940 onwards – concentration camps among them, including Auschwitz. Its representatives visited the camps in person to carry out risk assessments. After Kristallnacht in 1938, Allianz blocked payouts to Jewish policyholders and directed the money to the Nazi state instead. Decades later, Holocaust era survivors pursued the company through the courts. Allianz eventually agreed to settle the outstanding claims.

Then the conversation turned, unexpectedly, to football. In 2007 Allianz became the shirt sponsor of Górnik Zabrze, one of Poland's most storied clubs. The stadium Górnik played in had been built in 1934 in what was then German Hindenburg – now Zabrze – and was originally named the Adolf-Hitler-Kampfbahn. The Hitler name fell out of everyday use after the war but was never formally replaced until 2004, meaning that throughout Górnik's greatest years – their championships in the 1960s and 1980s and their 1970 European Cup Winners' Cup final against Manchester City – they were technically playing in a stadium still named after Hitler.

The Allianz sponsorship lasted two seasons, ending when Górnik were relegated in 2009.

The past does not always announce itself. Sometimes it turns up on a football shirt.
