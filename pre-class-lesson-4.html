Topic one

The companies that helped Hitler

Profit, complicity, and the silence that followed

The story of how some of America's biggest companies — Ford, General Motors, IBM, Standard Oil, Coca-Cola, ITT — helped finance, equip, and enable the Nazi regime is well-documented but rarely discussed in polite corporate history. Henry Ford was an open antisemite whose writings directly influenced Hitler; his portrait hung in Hitler's office. In 1938, a grateful Hitler awarded Ford the Order of the German Eagle, the most prestigious decoration that could be granted to a non-German.

IBM's punch-card technology helped the Nazis identify and track Jews across occupied Europe. Each concentration camp operated a Hollerith department using IBM machines to catalogue the movement, sorting, and mass execution of inmates. GM and Ford between them controlled 70% of the German car market by 1939 and built the trucks and engines that made the Blitzkrieg possible. After D-Day, American soldiers were bemused to find Ford and GM engines in the German vehicles they captured in Normandy.

The US Ambassador to Nazi Germany, William Dodd, wrote to President Roosevelt in 1936 that "more than a hundred American corporations have subsidiaries here." Standard Oil was supplying synthetic fuel technology. DuPont had intimate links with IG Farben, the chemical corporation centrally involved in Nazi rearmament and later implicated in the Holocaust. ITT owned a quarter of Focke-Wulf, the Luftwaffe aircraft manufacturer — and after the war, successfully claimed $27 million in compensation for Allied bombing damage to the plant.

These companies chose profit over principle — but they also operated in a legal grey zone where none of this was technically illegal. When the full horror of the Holocaust was revealed, they concocted myths of innocence. The country was only too happy to believe them.

Vocabulary

Doing business with Hitler

complicity

współudział

Involvement in or knowledge of wrongdoing, especially as a partner or accomplice.

The question is not just whether these companies knew, but the depth of their complicity.

to turn a blind eye

przymykać oko

To deliberately ignore something you know is wrong.

Washington turned a blind eye to the dealings because the profits were too large to question.

to divest (from)

wycofać się z inwestycji

To sell off investments or withdraw financial involvement from a company or country.

None of these corporations chose to divest from Germany, even after the persecution of Jews became public.

rearmament

zbrojenia, remilitaryzacja

The process of building up a country's military strength again, especially after a period of disarmament.

German rearmament in the 1930s was funded in part by American capital and technology.

subsidiary

spółka zależna, filia

A company controlled by a larger parent company, especially one operating in a foreign country.

Ford's German subsidiary, Ford-Werke in Cologne, produced military vehicles for the Wehrmacht.

forced labour / slave labour

praca przymusowa / praca niewolnicza

Work extracted from people under threat of punishment, without consent or fair compensation.

Ford's Cologne plant used forced labour between 1941 and 1945, including prisoners of war.

to collude (with)

działać w zmowie

To cooperate secretly with someone for a dishonest or illegal purpose.

Several US firms colluded with the Nazi regime to suppress competition and divide markets.

war profiteering

czerpanie zysków z wojny

Making excessive profits from the sale of goods or services during wartime, especially at the expense of others.

After the war, GM was accused of war profiteering on both sides of the conflict.

to whitewash

wybielac

To deliberately conceal unpleasant or incriminating facts about a person or organisation to protect their reputation.

Corporate histories have systematically whitewashed the extent of American involvement with the Nazis.

carte blanche

wolna ręka, pełna swoboda działania

Complete freedom to act as one wishes, without restriction. [from French, used in English]

Hitler gave German subsidiaries of US firms virtual carte blanche to operate as they wished, so long as they served the war effort.

moral reckoning

rozliczenie moralne

A moment when a person or institution is forced to confront the ethical consequences of past actions.

For most of these corporations, the moral reckoning never came — they simply moved on.

to bankroll

finansować, sponsorować

To provide the money for a project, enterprise, or activity, especially one that is large or controversial. [informal]

American banks effectively bankrolled Hitler's rise to power through loans and investments in German industry.

plausible deniability

wiarygodna zaprzeczalność

The ability to deny knowledge of or involvement in something because there is no direct proof linking you to it.

The corporate structure of subsidiaries gave headquarters in the US a degree of plausible deniability.

to fall into line

podporządkować się, dostosować się

To conform to expected behaviour or comply with the demands of those in power.

When the Nazis asked US subsidiaries to dismiss Jewish employees, most companies fell into line without protest.

Topic two

Talking in circles: the language of LinkedIn

Corporate jargon, status performance, and what people actually mean

A new feature of the Kagi translation app has been making people laugh — and wince. Called LinkedIn Speak, it translates plain English into the kind of inflated, jargon-heavy language that dominates the professional networking site. Type in "I saw a lovely donkey" and it gives you back: "I recently had a powerful reminder that consistent effort and carrying the heavy loads often go unrecognised, yet they are the backbone of any successful operation." The app also works in reverse: paste in a real LinkedIn post and it renders it back in plain English.

The joke lands because the target is so recognisable. LinkedIn has developed a house style that is less about communication and more about performance — projecting ambition, resilience, and thought leadership to an audience of potential employers and clients. Words like "leverage," "synergy," "pivot," and "stakeholder" have been used so often, and so loosely, that they have lost most of their meaning. The result is a kind of professional gibberish: language that sounds serious while saying very little.

For non-native English speakers, this creates a particular trap. Corporate jargon can feel like the language of competence — a code you need to learn to be taken seriously. In fact, the opposite is usually true. The clearer and more direct your English, the more authority you project. Plain English is not a failure of sophistication. It is sophistication.

The Guardian piece that prompted this topic is light in tone, but the underlying question is serious: when language is routinely used to obscure rather than communicate, what does that do to professional culture — and to trust?

Vocabulary

Talking in circles

jargon

żargon, nowomowa branżowa

Specialised language used by a particular group or profession, often difficult for outsiders to understand; used pejoratively when the language obscures rather than clarifies.

The report was full of management jargon that made it almost impossible to understand what was actually being proposed.

buzzword

modne słowo, słowo-klucz

A fashionable word or phrase, especially in a professional context, used more to signal membership of a group than to convey precise meaning. [informal, often pejorative]

"Synergy" has become such a tired buzzword that using it unironically now signals a lack of original thinking.

gibberish

bzdury, bełkot, bezsensowna gadanina

Rapid, unintelligible, or meaningless talk or writing; nonsense dressed up as communication. [informal]

She read the memo twice and concluded it was pure gibberish — impressive-sounding, but entirely empty.

to gloss over

przemilczać, zbagatelizować

To pass over something awkward or unflattering with a superficial or evasive treatment, avoiding proper scrutiny.

The press release glossed over three consecutive quarters of losses with talk of "strategic repositioning."

to leverage

wykorzystywać, używać jako d&zstrok;wigni

To use something to maximum advantage; in corporate language, applied so broadly it has become almost meaningless. [business jargon when overused]

The proposal claimed the company would leverage its existing relationships — a phrase that told the committee nothing at all.

thought leadership

pozycja eksperta, autorytet myślowy

The practice of positioning oneself as an authoritative expert in a field through published ideas; frequently used as self-promotion dressed as insight. [business / often ironic]

His LinkedIn profile described him as a "thought leader in digital transformation," which appeared to mean he had once given a talk at a conference.

to pivot

zmienić kierunek, przestawić się

To shift strategy or direction in response to changed circumstances; originally startup language, now used to describe almost any change, however minor. [business / often overused]

When the product failed, the CEO announced they would pivot — a word that covered everything from a minor adjustment to a complete reinvention.

to ostracise

wykluczać, izolować społecznie

To exclude someone from a group or from social or professional acceptance, often as a form of collective punishment.

Anyone who challenged the prevailing culture was quietly ostracised — not fired, just no longer invited to the meetings that mattered.

plain English

prosty, zrozumiały język

Language that is clear, direct, and accessible — free from unnecessary jargon or evasion. Used as a standard against which inflated professional language is measured.

The judge asked both sides to restate their arguments in plain English so the jury could actually follow them.

to cut through

przebijać się przez, rozciąć

To get past unnecessary complexity, evasion, or noise and reach the essential point directly.

A good cross-examination does not add complexity — it cuts through it, forcing the witness back to what was actually said or done.

to obscure

zaciemniać, ukrywać

To make something difficult to see, understand, or discover; to hide meaning, often deliberately, behind unclear or elaborate language.

The contract had been carefully drafted to obscure the clause that limited the client's right to compensation.

status signalling

sygnalizowanie statusu, manifestowanie pozycji

The use of language, behaviour, or objects to communicate one's social or professional standing to others, often more than to actually convey information.

Much of what passes for professional communication on LinkedIn is less about information and more about status signalling — telling your network how well things are going.

to wince

wzdrygać się, krzywić się

To make a slight involuntary grimace or shrinking movement in response to something painful, embarrassing, or awkward.

She winced as she read her own email from three years ago — the jargon was impenetrable even to her now.
